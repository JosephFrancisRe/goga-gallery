My Opinion Page

My story and instant hook:

It was the year 2023, I had finished playing the amazing game of "Elden Ring" and quickly had the motivation to search for another bussin' game to play, and thankfully I had stumbled across one, "Red Dead Redemption". I remember myself reading some comments on the first Red Dead Redemption and was instantly intrigued, I had asked my parents to get it for me and low and behold it was at my desk. I didnt hesitate to open the package, and had already inserted the disc into my xbox 1.

Things I liked about both RDR1 and RDR2:

In my opinion, Red Dead Redemption I and Red Dead Redemption II are two of the greatest video games ever made. They perfectly capture the spirit of the Wild West while telling emotional stories about loyalty, freedom, and redemption. What makes these games truly amazing is how they balance deep storytelling with spontaneous, funny moments that make the world feel alive and unpredictable. One minute you’re in the middle of a powerful scene about life and death, and the next, your horse trips over a small rock and launches you face-first into a cactus. That’s the magic of Red Dead Redemption it can make you laugh, cry, and think, all at the same time.

Gameplay Experience:

Both Red Dead Redemption games stand out because of how immersive and cinematic they feel. RDR2 in particular raises the bar for realism everything from the weather to the way your horse reacts to danger feels carefully designed. Yet, for all its beauty and emotional depth, it never takes itself too seriously. There’s always room for chaos and humor. For example, I’ve been moved by Arthur Morgan’s quiet reflections on loyalty, only to immediately ruin the moment by accidentally walking into someone’s horse and starting a crazy ah fistfight in Valentine. Or when I was admiring that beautiful sunset, lost in thought, and a random cougar decides I looked like dinner. The way the game shifts between emotional storytelling and absurd chaos, makes every playthrough unforgettable. The characters in both games are some of the most believable and human in all of gaming. Arthur Morgan’s story in Red Dead Redemption II is full of emotion he starts out as a hardened outlaw but slowly becomes a man searching for redemption and peace. John Marston’s journey in Red Dead Redemption I picks up that same theme, showing a man trying to escape his violent past and build a better life for his family. Both stories are tragic, powerful, and unforgettable. But even in the middle of all that seriousness, the game gives us moments that make the characters feel real and often hilarious. Arthur’s dry sarcasm and sharp wit are constant highlights. His casual insults “You don’t look so tough now!” and his sarcastic tone with strangers never fails to amuse me. Then there’s Uncle, who spends half his time at camp sleeping or complaining while everyone else does the work. His laziness becomes a running joke that makes camp life feel more authentic.

Favorite Characters RDR1:

John Martson, the protagonist, is relatable because he’s trying to leave his past behind and do right by his family. He’s strong and determined but also human, he makes mistakes, struggles with morality, and has emotional moments, especially concerning Abigail and Jack. You grow attached to him because his story is about redemption and protecting the people he loves.

Uncle is funny ,lazy, sarcastic, and always making snide comments, but he also shows loyalty and care for the family. His humor keeps the tone lighter, which makes the story feel more balanced emotionally.

Favorite Characters RDR2:

Arthur Morgan, the protagonist is a complex, relatable character. He’s tough and loyal, but he also struggles with morality and regrets, which makes him feel human. You see him grow throughout the story, forming friendships, protecting the gang, and even reflecting on his past mistakes. His humor and sarcasm, especially in camp conversations, make him charming, and moments like helping Lenny or joking with Uncle show his softer side.

Lenny Summers is young, smart, and funny. He’s loyal to the gang but still curious about the world. He brings energy and humor, like in the drunken mission A Quiet Time. I was shocked when the mission "Saint Denis bank robbery" was the moment when he died. That scene made me care about him even more. He’s a character that balances comic relief with genuine courage and intelligence

Uncle is seen once again as the gang’s comic relief. He's lazy, sarcastic, and always joking but he’s also loyal. He may complain about work, but he steps up when it matters, showing hidden heart. His humor adds personality to the gang and makes camp life feel real and fun.

Funny moments:

You could be in the middle of a gunfight and suddenly a bear strolls into the saloon or a raccoon steals your hat. Watching townspeople scream and panic while John casually stands there is hilarious.

Stranger missons like "Rabbit Hunt" involves rabbits zigzag unpredictably, sometimes running into water or cliffs. You might chase one for 5 minutes, only for it to disappear into thin air. The absurd persistence required is unintentionally funny.

Arthur’s horses have minds of their own. You try to sneak into a town quietly, but your horse spooks at a puddle, throws Arthur into a fence, and causes a chain reaction where townspeople freak out and NPCs start shooting each other. It’s chaotic and goofy.

NPC AI is unpredictable, like when A drunk man challenges Arthur to a fistfight… then trips over his own legs or when a random person gets stuck in a doorway while yelling at Arthur, and also when an NPC insults Arthur for no reason, then gets run over by a wagon

Using explosives or guns in tight spaces can produce goofy chaos, like for example, when you throw a stick of dynamite, it hits a barrel, barrels roll into the river, and a horse jumps in to chase them.

You have to round up a bunch of runaway sheep, but its so difficult. Sheep scatter unpredictably, jump into rivers, or run over your horse, making a simple herding task hilariously chaotic.

In the “The Sheep and the Goats” / “The Iniquities of History” missions, NPC's sometimes monologue about morality or insult you while you just try to get the mission done, creating unintentionally comedic moments.

Sad Moments:

After completing everything in RDR1, John Martson is gunned down by government agents while trying to surrender peacefully. The sense of helplessness and the music make it gut-wrenching. This scene made the climax of the game tragic and inevitable.

In the epilogue, Jack Marston seeks revenge on Edgar Ross. The cycle of violence and loss feels heavy, showing that John’s death reverberates for his family.

Early in the game, John must leave the people who helped him. Bonnie treated John like family, and leaving her ranch to chase his past mistakes creates a subtle but emotional tension.

Several minor characters die off-screen or are betrayed. NPCs who helped in side missions sometimes die in the background, making the world feel grim and real.

If you have high honor, he dies peacefully watching the sunrise; if low honor, he dies in pain and despair. Watching Arthur reflect on his life before dying is emotionally devastating.

Watching former friends betray each other, get killed, or leave due to mistrust (like Hosea, Lenny, or Micah’s manipulation) is heartbreaking, especially after growing attached to them.

Abigail worries constantly about Jack’s safety and the gang’s collapse, reflecting the human cost of Arthur’s outlaw life.

“The Mercies of History” / “A Fine Night of Debauchery” are missions that deal with strangers thoughts on death, poverty, or failed dreams.

Lenny Summers is only in his early 20s and we suddenly see him die in the Saint Denis bank robbery mission while trying to help his friends. His death is tragic because of his youth, potential, and likable personality. Players spent time bonding with him in camp and on missions, which makes losing him hit hard. The chaos of the moment and the unfairness of his death amplify the emotional impact, symbolizing the gang’s inevitable collapse and the harsh realities of their world.

Horses in RDR2 gain bond levels. The higher the bond, the more personality and obedience they show. Losing a "high-bond" horse after hours of riding feels like losing a close companion, and sometimes the game gives a death cutscene or notification, making it emotional. I miss Jack (early-game horse name).
