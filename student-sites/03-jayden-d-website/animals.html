Meet Our Animals!

American Aligator

Habitat:Freshwater Swamp

Diet:Fish

Size:11 to 15 feet

Fun Fact: American Aligator can go through over 2,000 teeth in their lifetime

American Black Bear

Habitat:Forsets & Mountains

Diet:Fish & Berries

Size:6'7 & 250 Lbs

Fun Fact:Black Bears are excellent climbers with strong, curved claws and can even run up to 35 miles per hour

Cheetah

Habitat: Savannas & Grasslands

Diet: Gazella & Rodents

Size: 5 feet & 110 Lbs

Fun Fact:A cheetah 0 to 60 speed is the same as 2016 Porsche 911 GT3 RS

Deer

Habitat: Forest

Diet: Grass, Fruits, and Nuts

Size: 3 feet & 200 Lbs

Fun Fact:A deer can jump horizontally up to 30 feet in a single leep.

Elephant

Habitat: Savannas & Grassland

Diet: Plants and Fruits

Size: 13 feet & 6 tons

Fun Fact:A elephant is the only mammals that cannot jump, and they have a very slow pulse of just 27 beats per minute.

Flamingo

Habitat: Tropical Lagoons

Diet: Shrimp & Algae

Size: 5 feet & 8 Lbs

Fun Fact: Newly hatched flamingos have dark grey feathers known as downy feathers.

Giraffe

Habitat: Savannas & Grassland

Diet:Leaves

Size:18 feet & 2 tons

Fun Fact:A fun fact about giraffes is that each giraffe has a unique spot pattern, much like human fingerprints, no two are alike.

Hippos

Habitat:Rivers, lakes, and wetlands

Diet: Grass, herbs, and leaves

Size:14 feet & 4 tons

Fun Fact: Hippos cannot swim or float—they walk or stand on riverbeds. They can hold their breath for up to 6 minutes and have a reflex that allows them to surface to breathe while sleeping underwater.

Iberian Lynx

Habitat:Mediterranean scrubland & Open woodlands

Diet:European rabbits

Size:3 feet & 24 lbs

Fun Fact:Once the world’s most endangered cat with fewer than 100 individuals in 2002, the Iberian lynx has made a remarkable recovery due to conservation efforts. As of 2023, over 2,000 now live in the wild.

Jaguar

Habitat:rainforests, wetlands, and grasslands

Diet:Turtles, fish, and monkeys.

Size:3 feet & 175 lbs

Fun Fact:Once the world’s most endangered cat with fewer than 100 individuals in 2002, the Iberian lynx has made a remarkable recovery due to conservation efforts. As of 2023, over 2,000 now live in the wild.
