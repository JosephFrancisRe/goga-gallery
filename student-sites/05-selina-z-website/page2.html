Vultures

Multiple vulture species, ranging from the Red-headed vulture to the Bearded vulture.

The Red-Headed Vulture

It's scientific name, "Sarcogyps Calvus", derives from "flesh vulture" (Sarcogyps) in Greek and "bald" (Calvus) in Latin. This species is considered an old-world vulture (found in Europe, Asia, and Africa), mainly found in Asia and the Indian subcontinent. It has a distinct naked red-orange head depending on the vultures age. Currently this species is critically endangered.

The Palm-Nut Vulture

It's scientific name, "Gypohierax Angolensis", derives from the Greek works "vulture"(gups) and "hawk"(hierax). It's plumage (feathers) is nearly all white white black areas on it's wings and tail. This species was originally misplaced with eagles, resembling eagles in flight. This species is mostly found in the African continent, feeding off of the fruit of the oil palm, other fruits, and grain unlike other birds of prey.

The Bearded Vulture

It's scientific name, "Gypaetus Barbatus", derives from "eagle" or "vulture" (gypaetus) from ancient Greek and "bearded" (barbatus) in Latin. Unlike most other vultures, the bearded vulture does not have a bald head, having a waddling like manner of walking with it's large feet. The usual orange-rust like coloration of these vultures come from rubbing mud upon their body.
